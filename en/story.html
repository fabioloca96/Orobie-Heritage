About Me

My Story

A guide shaped by curiosity, more than by any single trail.

01 — Roots

Born at the foot of the Presolana

I grew up in Clusone, in the Val Seriana, with the Presolana visible from nearly every window of my childhood. These mountains were never just a backdrop — they were the place where most of what shaped me actually happened.

Guiding people through them now feels less like a job and more like a continuation of a conversation I started as a kid, the first time I picked up a rock, a leaf or a mushroom and asked someone what it was.

02 — Reading the Landscape

Geology, botany, mycology, fossils

I've never been able to look at a mountain as just a mountain. A scree slope is a geology lesson; a wildflower meadow is a calendar of the season; a patch of damp forest floor is, more often than not, an excuse to crouch down and check what mushrooms have come up overnight. I collect fossils the way other people collect souvenirs — proof that these peaks have a much longer story than the one we're walking through today.

None of this is about expertise for its own sake. It's about being able to answer the question every good hike eventually raises: what am I actually looking at?

03 — Sounds of the Valleys

The baghèt, the guitar, the trumpet

Bergamo's mountain valleys have their own instrument, the baghèt — a bagpipe that nearly disappeared over the last century and is now kept alive by a small handful of musicians. I'm one of them. I also play guitar and trumpet, but the baghèt is the one that ties directly back to this land: it was built to be played at festivals, weddings and gatherings, in villages much like the ones we pass through on our walks.

Heritage, to me, isn't only stone huts and old trails — it's also sound. Ask, and I might bring it along.

04 — Bread, Slowly

What grows, what's foraged, what's fermented

I bake my own bread with a sourdough starter I've kept alive for years, and I'm endlessly interested in the food traditions of these valleys — the cheeses, the preserves, the dishes built around whatever the season provides. Mycology and food overlap more than you'd expect: most of what I forage, eventually, ends up on a plate.

05 — A Day Job, Sort Of

I teach electronics — and I never really stopped teaching

During the week, I teach electronics to high school students. It's a different mountain to climb, but the skill is the same one I bring to every walk: taking something that looks complicated and making it click, without losing what makes it interesting in the first place.

Whatever we end up talking about on the trail — lichen, glaciers, mushrooms, or the right way to tune a baghèt — it all comes from the same place: these mountains, and wanting to understand them a little better every day.
