The Territory

The kind of thing you only find out if someone tells you.

01 — Habitat

From chestnut woods to bare rock

The Orobie aren't one mountain — they're many mountains stacked on top of each other. In a few hours of walking you pass from broadleaf and chestnut woods in the lower valley, through beech and larch forest, up to alpine pastures and scree above two thousand metres — each band with its own air, its own light, its own residents.

Chamois and ibex graze the higher slopes, marmots whistle from the rockfields, golden eagles circle above the ridges. The Bergamo Orobie Alps Park protects much of this mosaic of habitats: the higher you climb, the more everything changes.

02 — Flowering

A flower that exists only here

Among the limestone outcrops of Monte Alben grows Primula albenensis, a flower first described by science from these very mountains and which, as far as we know, isn't found anywhere else on the planet. It's the kind of discovery that changes how you look at a meadow.

But the whole Orobie spring and summer is really a calendar of blooms: gentians, edelweiss, rhododendrons, martagon lilies. Every month has its own flowers, and every flower has its own story.

03 — Villages

Stone villages, stories worth finding

Gromo, with its old forges and the weapons that for centuries carried its name, is recognised as one of Italy's Most Beautiful Villages. Clusone holds one of the oldest working astronomical clocks in Europe, still ticking on the façade of its town hall. Castione della Presolana, Valbondione, Lizzola, Bratto: every village has a square, a church, a dialect nickname that tells you something.

Walking through the Orobie also means stepping into these villages, not just passing by them.

04 — Traditions

Mountain pastures, cheese, and mushrooms in the basket

In summer the cattle still move up to the alpine pastures, where Formai de Mut, a DOP cheese born on these very meadows, is made. It's an old mountain economy, built on hard work and the seasons, that you can get a real feel for by spending a little time with the people who still do it.

And then there are the mushrooms: for many people here it's almost a second harvest, an outing into the woods repeated every autumn for generations, with unwritten rules about where to look and what to leave growing. That's heritage too, even if it never makes it onto a postcard.

05 — Events

A calendar set by the seasons

In Valbondione, on a handful of summer days, the Serio Falls — among the highest in Europe — are released and put on a show the dam normally holds back. In autumn, when the cattle come back down from the high pastures, many villages celebrate with festivals, markets and mountain-dairy products.

Mushroom festivals, patron-saint feasts, Christmas markets in the smaller villages: it's always worth asking what's on before you set off.

Whatever season you come in, there's always something particular to discover along the trail. You just need to know how to look — or have someone point it out to you.
